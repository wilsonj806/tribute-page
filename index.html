Ai Wei Wei

Artist, political activist, humanitarian

A Brief Biography

Ai Wei Wei is one of the most high-profile Chinese contemporary artists and activists today. Born in 1957 to the Chinese poet Ai Qing during Mao’s Cultural Revolution, he has spent most of his childhood with his exiled parents. After Mao Zedong’s death, his family returned to Beijing and in 1978 Ai Wei Wei joined the Beijing Film Academy where he studied animation. There, he and several other students started an avant garde art group called the Stars. Then in 1981 to 1993 he moved to the United States where he briefly attended the Parsons School of Design in New York City. It was there that he was exposed to the works of Marcel Duchamp, Andy Warhol, and Jasper Johns and started creating his own conceptual art from readymade objects.

He returned to China in 1993 due to his father’s illness and in 1999 built his first architectural project, a studio house. His interest in architecture led him to found the architecture studio FAKE Design in 2003 and in the next decade would create numerous works using a variety of media (video, visual, etc). In addition he would also use his works for his own political activism such as the 2008 documentary (Little Cheeks) that would show the citizen investigation into the casualties of the May 2008 Sichuan earthquake; or his 2017 work, Law Of The Journey which reflects the ongoing refugee crisis of Syria.

More information about Ai Wei Wei can be found on his Wikipedia Page

Quotes

“I think my stance and my way of life is my most important art,” he said. “Those other works might be collectible—something you can hang on the wall—but that’s just a conventional perspective. We shouldn’t do things a certain way just because Rembrandt did it that way. If Shakespeare were alive today, he might be writing on Twitter.”

“I tried both ways, but most of the time I want my voice to be heard... I think, as artists, to give just a gesture is not enough. The fight takes a real struggle. To give a moral kind of superiority shows a problem, because we have to see that we’re all together. The struggle makes the meaning. I prefer to have a real fight than withdraw from the fight.”
